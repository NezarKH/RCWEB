Data and Analysis for Survey #1

In our analysis of Survey #1, we found that the average age of respondents was 22.57, with a majority being assigned female at birth (73.7%). Existing research supports the idea that being born a specific sex corresponds with societal disadvantages or advantages. A significant portion (40%) of respondents were neutral about whether an isolated environment would impact self-perception of gender. The null hypothesis that isolated environments do not impact self-perception of gender was not rejected, but the correlation was weak and dependent on one's lived experience. These findings contribute to the ongoing conversation about the influence of societal factors on gender identity and expression.

Exploring Opinions and Perspectives on Gender

There is a wide range of opinions on whether being born a specific sex gives inherent advantages or disadvantages in society. Responses varied from strongly disagree (1) to strongly agree (5). This indicates that people have diverse perspectives on the impact of sex on societal opportunities and challenges.

Many respondents seem to agree that in an isolated environment without societal influence, their gender or way of identification would be different (responses are mostly in the 3 to 5 range).

A significant number of respondents agree that gender is a representation of the authentic self, and people become what they identify as, regardless of their assigned sex (responses mostly in the range of 3 to 5).

When asked if they blame their assigned sex for the way society has treated them, responses are varied, suggesting that some people may feel that their assigned sex has negatively impacted their experiences, while others may not attribute societal treatment to their sex.

Most respondents feel pressured to conform to society's gender definitions (responses are mostly in the range of 4 to 5).

There is strong agreement that gender is subjective, and objective analysis that relies on rigid definitions fails to properly understand gender (responses mostly in the range of 4 to 5).

Responses vary on whether transitioning could serve as an escape from social problems, suggesting that people's opinions on this matter are diverse.

Most respondents believe that defining their gender for themselves should be the first step in questioning its validity (responses are mostly in the range of 4 to 5).

A majority of respondents felt that the questions made them look at gender from a different perspective (responses are mostly in the range of 4 to 5).

Many respondents believe there is significant misinformation about gender out there (responses mostly in the range of 4 to 5).

Individual Experiences and Opinions on Gender

This data contains responses from individuals discussing their experiences with gender, their thoughts on gender as a social construct, and the factors that have influenced their gender expression. The respondents also share their opinions on self-exploration, conformity to society, and the importance of diversity. Key insights from this data include:

  • The understanding and experience of gender are highly individualistic and can be influenced by various factors such as upbringing, culture, and personal interests.
  • Many respondents view gender as a label or a social construct that may provide comfort or confusion in different situations.
  • Self-exploration is considered essential for personal growth and happiness.
  • Conforming to society is seen as a complex issue, with some respondents suggesting that conformity can be beneficial in certain situations while others argue against it.
  • Gender is often seen as separate from biological sex, with many respondents acknowledging that there is misinformation about the two concepts.
  • A majority of the respondents believe that people are treated unfairly based on their gender, often due to outdated societal beliefs and discrimination.
  • The importance of diversity is emphasized by several respondents, as it promotes understanding and empathy among people with different backgrounds and experiences.

The varying opinions on gender and its impact on society demonstrate the complexities and nuances of the subject. People have diverse perspectives on gender, and further exploration of these experiences can contribute to a more inclusive and understanding society.

Understanding Gender Expression and Identity

Our analysis of Survey #2 revealed that gender expression is influenced by a variety of factors, including culture, childhood experiences, and social interactions. This suggests that gender expression can be both dynamic, evolving over time, and static, representing one's position in relation to societal norms.

The inner representation of gender is crucial in understanding the self, as it reflects an individual's authentic identity. Connecting this to Jung's concept of individuation, we find that aspects of the persona have gendered characteristics, which are distinct from the authentic self.

In the context of Withers (2020), addressing psychological distress within the persona may not be the same as addressing distress within the self. It's essential to recognize that individuals who prioritize societal gain over their authentic selves may not fully understand or represent their true identities.

Withers (2020) does not provide an absolute characterization of the authentic transgender experience, as individual experiences and identities are diverse and nuanced. This emphasizes the importance of considering the complexities of gender expression and identity in discussions and analyses.

Connection to Withers (2020) Article

In light of the survey data, the findings appear to challenge the arguments presented in Withers (2020). The data supports the notion put forth by Herdzik (2021) that gender expression is a form of individuation and authentic self, rather than merely an attempt to evade psychological distress as suggested by Withers (2020). The majority of respondents (60%) in the second survey agreed that gender is a representation of the authentic self and that people become what they identify with, regardless of their assigned sex. This highlights the importance of individuation in the development of gender identities, as outlined by Herdzik (2021), and provides evidence against the idea that conformity to societal expectations is the primary driving force behind gender expression.

Conclusion

In conclusion, the survey data offers valuable insights into the topics discussed in the Withers (2020) article. These findings can be used to further our understanding of the subject matter and inform future research or policy decisions. The data shows that a significant portion of the surveyed population views gender as an integral part of their authentic selves, which supports the individuation perspective. This challenges Withers' argument that transgender individuals seek gender-affirming care mainly to evade psychological distress and highlights the need for a more nuanced understanding of gender expression and its connection to the authentic self.
