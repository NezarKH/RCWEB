Data and Analysis for Survey #1

In our analysis of Survey #1, we found that the average age of respondents was 22.57, with a majority being assigned female at birth (73.7%). Existing research supports the idea that being born a specific sex corresponds with societal disadvantages or advantages. A significant portion (40%) of respondents were neutral about whether an isolated environment would impact self-perception of gender. The null hypothesis that isolated environments do not impact self-perception of gender was not rejected, but the correlation was weak and dependent on one's lived experience. These findings contribute to the ongoing conversation about the influence of societal factors on gender identity and expression.

Exploring Opinions and Perspectives on Gender

There is a wide range of opinions on whether being born a specific sex gives inherent advantages or disadvantages in society. Responses varied from strongly disagree (1) to strongly agree (5). This indicates that people have diverse perspectives on the impact of sex on societal opportunities and challenges.

Many respondents seem to agree that in an isolated environment without societal influence, their gender or way of identification would be different (responses are mostly in the 3 to 5 range).

A significant number of respondents agree that gender is a representation of the authentic self, and people become what they identify as, regardless of their assigned sex (responses mostly in the range of 3 to 5).

When asked if they blame their assigned sex for the way society has treated them, responses are varied, suggesting that some people may feel that their assigned sex has negatively impacted their experiences, while others may not attribute societal treatment to their sex.

Most respondents feel pressured to conform to society's gender definitions (responses are mostly in the range of 4 to 5).

There is strong agreement that gender is subjective, and objective analysis that relies on rigid definitions fails to properly understand gender (responses mostly in the range of 4 to 5).

Responses vary on whether transitioning could serve as an escape from social problems, suggesting that people's opinions on this matter are diverse.

Most respondents believe that defining their gender for themselves should be the first step in questioning its validity (responses are mostly in the range of 4 to 5).

A majority of respondents felt that the questions made them look at gender from a different perspective (responses are mostly in the range of 4 to 5).

Many respondents believe there is significant misinformation about gender out there (responses mostly in the range of 4 to 5).
